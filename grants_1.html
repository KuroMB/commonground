Free Money
in St. Louis.

A plain-English guide to grants available for nonprofits and community organizations looking to activate vacant spaces, build maker spaces, and create family gathering places in the St. Louis region.

Free — with a catch. You gotta do a little work to make this city a better place.

Most nonprofits and community organizations doing good work in St. Louis don't know how much grant money is sitting there waiting for them. It's not hidden — it's just buried in government websites, foundation portals, and program names that don't make obvious what they're for.

This page is our attempt to fix that. We've compiled the most relevant funding sources for organizations looking to activate vacant spaces, build community gathering places, and create programming for families and youth in the St. Louis area.

We update this page as new funding cycles open. If you know of a source we've missed, tell us.

Note: Grant deadlines change annually. Always verify current deadlines and eligibility directly with the funding source before applying. This page was last updated May 2026.

City of St. Louis

City funding for community activation.

The City of St. Louis Community Development Administration (CDA) runs the most accessible grant programs for nonprofit space activation. These are federally-funded through HUD's CDBG and HOME programs, administered locally. The key is knowing which program fits your project.

City of St. Louis · CDA
Neighborhood Beautification & Green Community Spaces
$50,000–$100,000

The most directly relevant grant for community space activation. Supports vacant lot beautification, green infrastructure, public space activation, recreation projects, and community art. Open to 501(c)(3) nonprofits and neighborhood organizations. Funding period runs through December 2026.

Eligible501(c)(3) nonprofits, neighborhood associations

Arts, Culture & Community Programming

Funding for programs and people.

These sources fund the programming layer of community spaces — the events, workshops, arts activities, and cultural programming that turn a building into a destination. If your space activation includes an arts or community programming component, these are your best options.

Federal & State Programs

Bigger money,
more paperwork.

Federal and state programs require more effort but offer larger awards and longer funding periods. These are worth understanding even if you're not ready to apply yet — knowing they exist shapes how you design and frame your project from the start.

Federal · HUD
Community Development Block Grant (CDBG)
Varies by jurisdiction

The backbone of community development funding nationally. St. Louis City and County both administer CDBG funds separately. Eligible uses include community facilities, public services, and neighborhood improvements. If your project serves low-to-moderate income communities, CDBG is likely your largest available source.

EligibleNonprofits, government entities, CDCs
Access viaCity CDA or St. Louis County Human Services

Missouri · Tax Incentive
Tax Increment Financing (TIF)
Project-specific

TIF isn't a grant — it's a financing mechanism that redirects future property tax increases from a redevelopment area to fund the redevelopment itself. Relevant for larger projects involving property acquisition and significant buildout. Worth understanding if you're working with a developer or municipality on a larger space conversion.

Best forLarger projects with development partners
Access viaCity/County TIF commission
StatusProject-specific

How to Actually Get This Money

Grant writing tips from
someone who's read the applications.

Most grant applications fail not because the project is bad, but because the framing is wrong. Here's what we've learned about how to position community space projects for funding success.

01
Frame it as community infrastructure, not a building project

Grant reviewers fund outcomes, not construction. "We're renovating a vacant storefront" loses. "We're creating a free after-school maker space serving 150 youth in a food-insecure census tract" wins. Lead with people and outcomes, not square footage.

02
Know your census tract status before you apply

Most City and CDBG grants prioritize projects in Qualified Census Tracts (QCTs) and Economic Justice Index priority areas. Check the CDA maps before you write a single word. If your project is in a priority area, say so in your first paragraph.

03
Stack your funding sources — don't rely on one

A $50K CDA grant plus a $5K Walmart Spark Good grant plus a RAC Program Support grant plus in-kind donations is a $75K+ project. Grant reviewers like seeing diverse funding — it signals community buy-in and reduces their risk.

04
Start smaller than you think you need to

A $15K pilot that proves demand is worth more than a $200K plan that hasn't happened yet. The Lighter, Quicker, Cheaper approach — temporary programming, pop-up events, community workshops before permanent buildout — gives you real data for your next grant application.

05
Community engagement is evidence, not a checkbox

Documentation of community support — survey results, letters from neighborhood associations, attendance at public meetings — dramatically strengthens applications. Start collecting it early, before you need it for a grant deadline.

06
Read last year's awards list

Most government grant programs publish their award lists. Finding organizations that received funding for similar projects tells you exactly how to frame yours. The CDA publishes its Neighborhood Transformation Grant awards annually on its website.
