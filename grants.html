For Homeowners

Help for your home.

City, county, and federal programs help St. Louis homeowners with repairs, weatherization, and improvements — often at no cost to qualifying residents.

City of St. Louis · CDA
Healthy Home Repair Program
Grants & low-interest loans

The City's flagship home repair program for low-to-moderate income homeowners. Covers health and safety repairs — roofing, electrical, plumbing, structural issues. Administered by CDA in partnership with Mission: St. Louis. An evaluator assesses your home and determines eligibility.

IncomeLow to moderate income households

St. Louis County
St. Louis County Home Improvement Program
Up to $5,000 · No cost

Free home repairs for eligible low-to-moderate income homeowners in St. Louis County. Covers roofing, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, hot water heater, tuck-pointing, concrete, and more. Operated through the County's Department of Human Services.

Up to $10,000 grant + $40,000 loan

A $10,000 grant (never repaid unless you sell within 3 years) plus low-interest loans for major repairs. Best for homeowners outside major metro areas. In 2026, USDA is prioritizing homes with failing septic systems and private wells.

Rainscaping & Green Space

Get paid to go greener.

St. Louis has real money for rainscaping and native plantings — and unlike most grants, these are accessible to individual homeowners and small organizations with rolling applications and no hard deadlines.

MSD Project Clear · Open Now
Rainscaping Small Grants Program
Up to $4,000

One of the most accessible grants in St. Louis — open to homeowners, businesses, and nonprofits with rolling applications and no hard deadlines. Covers rain gardens, native plantings, rain barrels, permeable pavement, green roofs, and bioswales. Part of MSD's $120M Project Clear initiative to reduce sewer overflows and basement flooding. Attend a free orientation first, then apply. River des Peres area funds are waitlisted until July 2026.

Lower your bills.

How to Actually Get This Money

Grant writing tips from
someone who's read the applications.

Most applications fail not because the project is bad, but because the framing is wrong.

01
Frame it as outcomes, not a building project

Grant reviewers fund people, not construction. "We're creating a free after-school maker space serving 150 youth in a food-insecure census tract" wins. "We're renovating a storefront" loses.

02
Know your census tract before you apply

Most City and CDBG grants prioritize Qualified Census Tracts and Economic Justice Index areas. Check the CDA maps first. If you're in a priority area, say so in paragraph one.

03
Stack your sources — don't rely on one

A $50K CDA grant + $5K Walmart Spark Good + RAC Program Support + in-kind donations = a $75K+ project. Grant reviewers like diverse funding — it reduces their risk.

04
Start smaller than you think you need to

A $15K pilot that proves demand is worth more than a $200K plan that hasn't happened yet. Test the concept cheaply first, then use the data in your next application.

05
Community engagement is evidence

Letters from neighborhood associations, survey results, meeting attendance records — collect them early. They dramatically strengthen applications and show you're not working in a vacuum.

06
Read last year's award list

Most programs publish who they funded and why. Reading prior awards tells you exactly how to frame yours. CDA publishes Neighborhood Transformation Grant awards annually on their site.
